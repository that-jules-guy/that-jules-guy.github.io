I went down a thread today on why movie punches sound more like punches than actual punches do.

The recipe is messier than I expected. A movie punch is a stack: celery snapped close to a mic for the cartilage crack, raw chicken slapped on a leather jacket for the meat-thud, a wet leather glove driven into a phone book for padded mid-body, a hand clap on top for transient attack. Then mid-frequency boost around 200–400 Hz for weight, a sub-bass layer dropped an octave, gentle compression. Gary Hecker keeps a refrigerator on his foley stage specifically for produce. John Roesch at Skywalker has talked about punching slabs of meat wrapped in chamois.

The reason an actual fist-to-face recording sounds underwhelming is that an actual fist-to-face *is* underwhelming as a sonic event. A short dull thump, mid-range, no transient, no low end. The ear in a real fight fills in violence from adrenaline and visual context. A microphone has neither.

Gunshots are worse. Skip Lievsay, mixing *No Country for Old Men*, said the actual location-recorded shotgun sounded "like a balloon popping." The cinematic gunshot we all know is built from cannon fire, dumpster slams, mechanical click-clacks, and pitched-down 808s, with a tail of slap-back recorded in a canyon. Real gunshots are 140-plus dB of broadband transient that microphones can't faithfully capture. What audiences think a gun sounds like is a Hollywood composite that has trained two generations of ears.

There's writing on the recursion. Michel Chion calls it **rendered sound** — sound that conveys the *idea* of an event, not its acoustic reality, and which becomes the cultural reference for the event. Walter Murch frames it as **encoded vs. embodied**: cinema sound is encoded shorthand, and once shorthand dominates, the embodied reality sounds wrong. Audiences reject accurate sound as fake.

I had this tied up neatly — *movies trained our ears, real things now sound wrong because of it* — and then someone close to me described being near actual gunfire recently. They thought it was firecrackers. Everyone around them thought it was firecrackers. It took people running for anyone to call it what it was.

I assumed the schema didn't fire. They corrected me: it absolutely did. Marvel had trained them to expect *deep* and *theatrical* — that's what gunfire was supposed to sound like. What they actually heard was *sharp* and *fast*. Sharp and fast didn't match the deep-theatrical template, so the brain rejected "gunshot" and went looking for the nearest *other* thing that sounds sharp and fast. Cherry bomb fits perfectly. So does a firecracker. So does a car backfire.

That's a sharper version of the recursion than I had. The Marvel-trained schema didn't fail to fire. It fired and *actively prevented identification*, because the schema had encoded the wrong sonic shape. Real gunshots are sharp and fast. The cinematic boom is the lie. The ears were trained to recognize the lie, and when the truth arrived they routed it to the harmless cabinet — because nothing in the cabinet labeled "gunshot" looked anything like the truth.

The reframe is unkind, if you sit with it. The trained schema isn't just an alternate to the real one. It can be specific enough that it *blocks* the real one. Knowing the movie version of a thing means you've also been trained to misidentify the actual thing when it shows up, because your brain is checking against the template you've practiced, and the template is shaped wrong.

There's no genre called Tuesday afternoon. There's no Marvel scene where gunfire is sharp and fast and indistinguishable from a kid's firework. So when reality arrives in that shape, it has nowhere to go that says "this." It goes to the closest thing that says something else.

Randy Thom said foley's job is the feeling of the event, not the event. He meant it as craft. It's also how the rest of us get through most days — pattern-matching the feeling, until the feeling doesn't fit, and the room starts running.
